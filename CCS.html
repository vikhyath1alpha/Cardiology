1. Definition and Classification of Chronic Coronary Syndromes

1.1 ESC 2024 Definition

The 2024 European Society of Cardiology (ESC) Guidelines define Chronic Coronary Syndromes (CCS) as a range of clinical presentations arising from structural and/or functional alterations related to chronic diseases of the coronary arteries and/or microcirculation 5 380. These alterations lead to a temporary, reversible mismatch between myocardial oxygen demand and blood supply, resulting in myocardial hypoperfusion and ischemia.

Key Evolution from 2019 Guidelines

  • • Expanded pathophysiological concepts to include microvascular disease and vasospastic disorders
  • • Alignment with 2023 AHA/ACC terminology shift to "chronic coronary disease (CCD)"
  • • Emphasis on stepwise diagnostic and therapeutic approach

1.2 Clinical Spectrum and Presentations

Five Key Patient Populations

  1. 1. Nonacute patients post-ACS or revascularization
  2. 2. Patients with heart failure of ischemic/cardiometabolic origin
  3. 3. Symptomatic patients with reproducible stress-induced angina
  4. 4. Patients with ANOCA/INOCA (angina/ischemia without obstructive CAD)
  5. 5. Asymptomatic patients with abnormal test results

ANOCA/INOCA Emphasis

Angina with No Obstructive Coronary Arteries (ANOCA) and Ischemia with No Obstructive Coronary Arteries (INOCA) represent significant advancements in understanding CCS pathophysiology.

The 2024 ESC guidelines place increased emphasis on diagnosing and managing these conditions 416.

2. Pathophysiology of Chronic Coronary Syndromes

The pathophysiology of CCS is complex and multifactorial, involving both structural and functional abnormalities in epicardial coronary arteries and coronary microcirculation 404.

2.1 Epicardial CAD

Characterized by atherosclerotic plaque accumulation in large coronary arteries, leading to luminal narrowing and reduced blood flow during increased myocardial demand.

Chronic inflammatory process
Lipid accumulation & macrophage infiltration
Vulnerable plaque formation

2.2 Endothelial Dysfunction

Critical early event in atherosclerosis pathogenesis, particularly in ANOCA/INOCA patients, involving imbalance between vasodilating and vasoconstricting factors.

Diagnostic Gold Standard: Acetylcholine provocation testing to assess endothelial function 385

2.3 Microvascular Dysfunction

Structural/functional abnormalities in pre-arterioles and arterioles (<500 µm) impairing myocardial blood flow regulation.

Coronary Flow Reserve (CFR): <2.0-2.5 suggests CMD
Index of Microcirculatory Resistance (IMR): Elevated values indicate dysfunction

2.4 Coronary Vasospasm and Plaque Vulnerability

Vasospastic Angina

Transient, intense constriction of epicardial coronary artery segments, potentially leading to complete occlusion and myocardial ischemia.

Associated with hyperreactivity of vascular smooth muscle cells and endothelial dysfunction 388.

Plaque Vulnerability

Vulnerable plaques characterized by thin fibrous cap, large lipid-rich necrotic core, positive remodeling, and significant inflammation.

Many acute events arise from rupture of previously non-obstructive plaques 391.

3. Diagnosis of Chronic Coronary Syndromes

ESC 2024: Four-Step Diagnostic Approach

1

3.1 General Clinical Examination

Comprehensive Assessment
  • • Detailed symptom history (beyond typical/atypical classification)
  • • Differential diagnosis consideration
  • • Physical examination for CVD signs
Basic Testing
  • • 12-lead resting ECG
  • • Blood tests (lipid profile, glucose, renal function)
  • • hs-CRP/fibrinogen (Class IIa, Level B)
2

3.2 Pre-test Clinical Likelihood Assessment

Risk Factor-Weighted Clinical Likelihood (RF-CL) Model

Class I, Level B Incorporates age, sex, symptom characteristics, and cardiovascular risk factors 380.

Key Impact: Tripled the number of individuals classified as very low likelihood (≤5%), reducing unnecessary testing
Additional Evaluations
  • • Transthoracic Echocardiography (TTE)
  • • Exercise ECG testing (selected patients)
  • • Ambulatory ECG monitoring (suspected vasospasm)
3

3.3 Confirming the Diagnosis

Anatomical Imaging

CCTA preferred for low-intermediate PCL (>5% to ≤50%)

High negative predictive value, assesses plaque burden and high-risk features

Functional Imaging
  • • Stress Echocardiography
  • • Myocardial Perfusion SPECT/PET
  • • Stress Cardiac MRI
Invasive Tests
  • • Invasive Coronary Angiography (ICA)
  • • Functional Assessment (FFR, iFR)
  • • Coronary Function Testing
4

3.4 Initial Therapy and Follow-up

Initial Therapy

Intensification of lifestyle modifications, risk factor management, and guideline-directed medical therapy (GDMT)

Follow-up Considerations

Repeat revascularization for persistent symptoms despite optimized GDMT, functional testing for uncertain symptoms

4. Management of Chronic Coronary Syndromes

4.1 Guideline-Directed Medical Therapy (GDMT)

4.1.1 Lifestyle Modifications & Risk Factor Control

Core Interventions
  • • Smoking cessation programs
  • • Mediterranean or DASH diet
  • • Regular physical activity (≥150 min moderate-intensity/week)
  • • Weight management for overweight/obese patients
Mobile Health Interventions

Class I, Level C Text messages, apps, wearable devices to improve adherence 151

4.1.2 Antiplatelet Therapy

ESC 2024 Updates

Clopidogrel monotherapy (75mg daily) Class I, Level A for CCS patients with prior MI or remote PCI

Supported by HOST-EXAM trial showing superiority over aspirin 131

High Bleeding Risk Patients

Short DAPT duration (1-3 months) recommended based on Master-DAPT trial 486

4.1.3 Lipid-Lowering Therapy

ESC 2024 Targets

Primary Goal: LDL-C <1.4 mmol/L (55 mg/dL) and ≥50% reduction from baseline

Class I, Level A High-intensity statins up to maximum tolerated dose 160

Add ezetimibe if targets not met
Consider PCSK9 inhibitors if still not achieved
Bempedoic acid for statin-intolerant patients
ACC/AHA 2023 Approach

High-intensity statins recommended. If contraindicated:

Moderate-intensity statins targeting 30-49% LDL-C reduction 346

4.2 Novel Therapeutic Agents

SGLT2 Inhibitors

Class I, Level A Recommended for CCS patients with T2DM to reduce CV events

• Proven CV benefits in DECLARE-TIMI 58, EMPA-REG OUTCOME

• Benefits extend beyond glucose control

GLP-1 Receptor Agonists

Class I, Level A for T2DM
Class IIa, Level B for obesity without diabetes

• SELECT trial: Semaglutide reduced MACE in obese patients without diabetes

• Weight reduction and metabolic benefits

Colchicine

Class IIb, Level A Low-dose colchicine (0.5mg daily) considered in CCS patients with atherosclerotic CAD

• LoDoCo2 trial: 31% RRR in composite endpoint

• Targets residual inflammatory risk

4.3 Revascularization Strategies

Percutaneous Coronary Intervention (PCI)

Indication: Symptomatic CCS patients with functionally significant obstructive CAD (FFR ≤0.80) who remain symptomatic despite GDMT

ORBITA-2 trial reinforced PCI efficacy for symptom relief 355

Intracoronary Imaging Guidance: Class I for complex lesions (left main, bifurcations, long lesions)

Coronary Artery Bypass Grafting (CABG)

Preferred for: Complex multivessel disease, left main disease, diabetes, LV dysfunction

STICH trial: CABG superior to medical therapy in ischemic cardiomyopathy with LVEF ≤35%

Heart Team Approach: Class I, Level C Multidisciplinary decision-making essential

5. Recent Clinical Trials and Evolving Evidence (2022-2025)

5.1 Trials Informing Medical Therapy

Antiplatelet Therapy

HOST-EXAM Trial

Clopidogrel vs Aspirin monotherapy post-PCI

Clopidogrel superior in reducing composite endpoint (5.7% vs 7.7%, HR 0.73)

Master-DAPT Trial

Abbreviated vs Standard DAPT in HBR patients

1-month DAPT non-inferior for net adverse clinical events, superior for bleeding

Novel Therapeutic Agents

LoDoCo2 Trial

Low-dose colchicine (0.5mg) in chronic coronary disease

31% reduction in composite CV endpoint (HR 0.69)

SELECT Trial

Semaglutide in overweight/obese adults with CVD, no diabetes

20% reduction in MACE (CV death, MI, stroke)

5.2 Trials Informing Revascularization

PCI Optimization

ORBITA-2 Trial

PCI vs Sham in stable angina without antianginals

PCI significantly improved angina symptoms and exercise tolerance

RENOVATE-COMPLEX-PCI

IVUS-guided vs angiography-guided PCI

38% reduction in composite endpoint with IVUS guidance

Special Populations

REVIVED-BCIS2 Trial

PCI + OMT vs OMT alone in ischemic LV dysfunction (LVEF ≤35%)

No reduction in death or HF hospitalization with PCI

ISCHEMIA-EXTEND

Long-term follow-up of ISCHEMIA trial cohorts

No mortality difference between invasive vs conservative strategies

5.3 ANOCA/INOCA Trials

CorMicA Trial

Stratified medical therapy based on invasive coronary function testing improved angina symptoms

WARRIOR Trial

Intensive guideline-based cardioprotective regimen vs usual care in women with ischemia, no obstructive CAD

PRIZE Trial

Zibotentan (endothelin receptor antagonist) in patients with microvascular angina

6.3 ESC 2024 Diagnostic Approach for Suspected CCS

Step 1: General Clinical Examination

Detailed symptom assessment, physical examination, 12-lead ECG, basic blood tests

Step 2: Pre-test Likelihood Assessment

TTE, RF-CL model, exercise ECG in selected patients

Step 3: Confirming Diagnosis

CCTA for low-intermediate PCL, functional imaging, invasive tests as needed

Step 4: Initial Therapy & Follow-up

GDMT optimization, revascularization for persistent symptoms
