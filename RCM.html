1. Definition & Classification

Core Definition

Restrictive cardiomyopathy is characterized by impaired ventricular filling due to increased myocardial stiffness, leading to diastolic dysfunction, while systolic function is typically preserved in early stages .

1.2 Primary RCM

Intrinsic myocardial disease, often genetic

  • • Sarcomeric gene mutations: TNNI3, MYH7, TNNT2
  • • Cytoskeletal proteins: DES, FLNC, TTN
  • • Metabolic proteins: CRYAB
  • • Idiopathic forms

1.3 Secondary RCM

Systemic diseases affecting the heart

  • • Infiltrative: Amyloidosis, Sarcoidosis
  • • Storage: Hemochromatosis, Fabry disease
  • • Fibrotic: Radiation, Scleroderma
  • • Endomyocardial: EMF, Hypereosinophilia

Pediatric Considerations

In children, RCM accounts for less than 2.5-4.5% of pediatric cardiomyopathy cases , with a particularly poor prognosis - two-year survival less than 50% without transplantation .

3. Etiology & Pathophysiology

3.1 Genetic Mechanisms

Primary RCM often involves mutations affecting cardiac proteins:

  • ▸
    Sarcomeric genes: TNNI3 mutations increase Ca²⁺ sensitivity, causing diastolic dysfunction
  • ▸
    Cytoskeletal defects: DES mutations cause desmin aggregate accumulation
  • ▸
    Metabolic proteins: CRYAB mutations affect heat shock proteins

3.2 Pathophysiological Mechanisms

Increased ventricular stiffness
Impaired diastolic filling
Elevated ventricular pressures
Biatrial enlargement & congestion

3.3 Secondary Causes

Infiltrative Diseases

Amyloid fibrils deposit in extracellular matrix, physically restricting myocardial expansion

Storage Disorders

Iron (hemochromatosis) or glycosphingolipids (Fabry) accumulate within cardiomyocytes

Endomyocardial Fibrosis

Fibrous tissue deposition on endocardial surface, encasing papillary muscles

3.4 Common Pathological Processes

  • • Interstitial fibrosis
  • • Myocardial infiltration
  • • Storage material accumulation
  • • Endomyocardial thickening
  • • Cellular toxicity and dysfunction

4. Clinical Presentation

4.1 Cardinal Symptoms

  • Dyspnea on exertion (earliest symptom)
  • Exercise intolerance
  • Fatigue and weakness
  • Orthopnea & PND

4.2 Signs of Congestion

  • Jugular venous distension (JVD)
  • Peripheral edema
  • Ascites & hepatomegaly
  • Bibasilar crackles

4.3 Arrhythmias

  • Atrial fibrillation (most common)
  • Atrial flutter
  • Heart block (in infiltrative disease)
  • Thromboembolic events

Physical Examination Pearls

Key Findings

  • • Rapid 'y' descent in JVP
  • • Kussmaul sign (paradoxical JVP rise on inspiration)
  • • S3 or S4 gallop (less common than DCM)
  • • Functional AV valve regurgitation murmurs

4.5 Pediatric Presentation

  • • Poor feeding & failure to thrive
  • • Recurrent respiratory infections
  • • Non-specific fatigue and weakness
  • • Often rapid progression

5. Diagnostic Workup

5.1 Electrocardiography (ECG)

Biatrial enlargement: Tall P waves (II), deep negative P (V1)
Low voltage QRS: Especially in amyloidosis
Arrhythmias: AF, flutter, conduction abnormalities
Non-specific ST-T changes

5.3 Cardiac Catheterization

Hemodynamic Findings

  • • Elevated ventricular filling pressures
  • • "Dip-and-plateau" pattern (square root sign)
  • • LVEDP often > RVEDP by >5 mmHg
  • • Reduced cardiac output

5.2 Echocardiography

Structural Findings:
  • • Normal/near-normal ventricular sizes
  • • Preserved LV systolic function
  • • Biatrial enlargement
  • • Variable wall thickness
Doppler Features:
  • • High E wave, short deceleration time (<150 ms)
  • • Reduced e' velocity (septal e' < 7 cm/s)
  • • Elevated E/e' ratio (>14-15)
  • • Dilated IVC with reduced collapse
Advanced Techniques:
  • • Reduced global longitudinal strain (GLS)
  • • Speckle tracking abnormalities

5.4 Cardiac MRI (CMR)

Tissue characterization: Superior to echo for infiltration detection
Late Gadolinium Enhancement: Pattern-specific (subendocardial in amyloidosis)
Parametric mapping: T1 mapping, ECV quantification for fibrosis
Iron quantification: T2* for hemochromatosis

5.5 Endomyocardial Biopsy

Selective Role

Primarily when specific treatable etiology suspected and cannot be diagnosed non-invasively

•
Amyloidosis: Congo red with apple-green birefringence
•
Sarcoidosis: Non-caseating granulomas (low sensitivity)
•
Hemochromatosis: Prussian blue iron staining
•
Primary RCM: Non-specific fibrosis and hypertrophy

5.6 Genetic Testing

Increasing Importance

Crucial for primary/familial RCM - enables cascade screening and prognostic information

Gene Panels Include:
  • • Sarcomeric: MYH7, TNNT2, TNNI3, TPM1
  • • Cytoskeletal: DES, FLNC, TTN
  • • Metabolic: CRYAB
  • • Disease-specific: TTR, GLA

Interpretation complexity: Variants of uncertain significance (VUS) require correlation with clinical picture

6. Pathology

6.1 Histopathological Spectrum

Primary RCM

Non-specific findings dominate:

  • • Interstitial fibrosis (diffuse or focal)
  • • Myocyte hypertrophy
  • • Perimyocytic collagen lattice (EM)
  • • Desmin aggregate accumulation (DES mutations)

Electron Microscopy Features

In MYH7-related RCM: electron-dense materials from focal myocyte degeneration, tubular structures, pseudo-inclusion bodies

6.2 Disease-Specific Findings

Amyloidosis

Congo red-positive extracellular deposits with apple-green birefringence under polarized light

Sarcoidosis

Non-caseating granulomas with epithelioid histiocytes, giant cells, lymphocytes

Hemochromatosis

Prussian blue staining reveals iron granules within cardiomyocytes

Fabry Disease

Lysosomal glycosphingolipid accumulation with concentric lamellar bodies on EM

7. Differential Diagnosis

Critical Distinction

Differentiating RCM from constrictive pericarditis is paramount - constrictive pericarditis may be curable with pericardiectomy, while RCM management focuses on symptom relief and treating underlying causes.

7.2 RCM vs Constrictive Pericarditis

Feature Restrictive Cardiomyopathy Constrictive Pericarditis
Pathophysiology Intrinsic myocardial disease (stiffness) Pericardial disease (thickened/fibrotic pericardium)
Pericardium Normal thickness Often thickened (>3-4 mm)
Mitral Annular e' Reduced (septal e' < 7 cm/s) Preserved (septal e' > 8-9 cm/s)
Respiratory Variation Minimal (<15-25%) Significant (>25%)
LVEDP vs RVEDP LVEDP > RVEDP by >5 mmHg LVEDP ≈ RVEDP (within 5 mmHg)
Ventricular Interdependence Less pronounced Pronounced, discordant

7.3 Other Cardiomyopathies

  • •
    Hypertrophic (HCM): Significant LVH, different diastolic pattern
  • •
    Dilated (DCM): Systolic dysfunction, ventricular dilation
  • •
    Myocarditis: Acute/chronic inflammation leading to fibrosis

7.4 Ischemic & Hypertensive

  • •
    Coronary artery disease: Ischemia-induced diastolic dysfunction
  • •
    Hypertensive heart disease: Increased LV mass, impaired relaxation
  • •
    LVOT obstruction: Secondary effects on diastolic function

7.5 Systemic Conditions

  • •
    Scleroderma: Myocardial fibrosis
  • •
    Radiation therapy: Progressive myocardial fibrosis
  • •
    Neoplastic infiltration: Metastatic disease

8. Management

General Principles

Treatment is frequently unsatisfactory unless a specific, treatable underlying cause can be identified. Management is primarily supportive, focusing on symptom relief and complication management .

Standard heart failure medications (ACE inhibitors, ARBs, many beta-blockers) are generally poorly tolerated and may be harmful due to vasodilatory effects in preload-dependent patients.

8.2 Medical Management

Diuretics

Mainstay for fluid overload management

  • • Loop diuretics (furosemide) primary therapy
  • • Thiazides for resistant edema
  • • Careful titration required

Anticoagulation

For thromboembolic prevention

  • • Indicated for AF/flutter
  • • NOACs preferred over warfarin
  • • Consider in high-risk conditions

Antiarrhythmics

Arrhythmia management

  • • Rate control for AF (cautious beta-blockers)
  • • Amiodarone for rhythm control
  • • Catheter ablation in select cases

8.3 Device & Surgical Therapy

Device Therapy

  • • Pacemakers: Symptomatic bradyarrhythmias, AV block
  • • ICDs: Primary/secondary prevention for ventricular arrhythmias
  • • CRT: Generally not beneficial (preserved systolic function)

Cardiac Transplantation

Definitive treatment for end-stage disease

  • • Best chance for long-term survival
  • • Early referral essential
  • • Eligibility depends on comorbidities

8.4 Management of Specific Etiologies

9. Prognosis

9.1 General Prognosis: Often Poor

The overall prognosis for restrictive cardiomyopathy is generally poor due to progressive diastolic dysfunction and limited efficacy of medical therapies.

Pediatric RCM:
  • • Two-year survival <50% without transplantation
  • • Rapid progression typical
  • • High arrhythmia burden
Adult RCM:
  • • Untreated ATTR: 3-4 year median survival
  • • AL amyloidosis: More aggressive course
  • • Progression to advanced HF common

9.2 Factors Influencing Prognosis

Underlying etiology: Genetic forms, specific mutations
Diastolic dysfunction severity: Degree of ventricular stiffening
Arrhythmias: AF control, ventricular arrhythmias
Pulmonary hypertension: Reactive component development
RV dysfunction: Right heart failure presence
Age at diagnosis: Pediatric onset typically worse

9.3 Pediatric Specific Considerations

Clinical Challenges

  • • Non-specific symptoms (poor feeding, failure to thrive)
  • • Recurrent respiratory infections
  • • Often genetic basis (sarcomeric, DES mutations)
  • • Rapid progression to severe HF

High-Risk Features

  • • Life-threatening arrhythmias
  • • High sudden cardiac death risk
  • • Pulmonary hypertension development
  • • Thromboembolic complications

Management Priority

  • • Cardiac transplantation mainstay
  • • Early referral to pediatric HF centers
  • • Family screening and genetic counseling
  • • Timely evaluation and listing

9.4 Prognostic Improvement Strategies

Early Diagnosis

Multimodality imaging, genetic testing, advanced biomarkers

Targeted Therapy

Etiology-specific treatments, novel agents for amyloidosis

Transplant Timing

Early referral, appropriate patient selection criteria

10. Recent Advances & Future Directions

10.1 Diagnostic Imaging Advances

Cardiac MRI Innovation

  • • Native T1 mapping for diffuse fibrosis
  • • Extracellular volume (ECV) quantification
  • • Pattern recognition for specific etiologies
  • • Pre-LGE detection capabilities

Echocardiography Evolution

  • • Speckle-tracking echocardiography (STE)
  • • Myocardial work indices
  • • Subtle systolic dysfunction detection
  • • Strain imaging advancements

10.2 Novel Biomarkers

Serum Biomarkers

  • • Myocardial stress markers
  • • Fibrosis-related proteins
  • • Disease-specific markers (amyloidogenic chains)
  • • Risk stratification tools

Imaging Biomarkers

  • • Quantitative tissue characterization
  • • Disease progression monitoring
  • • Treatment response assessment
  • • Early detection capabilities

10.3 Evolving Management Strategies

Disease-Specific Therapies

•
ATTR amyloidosis: TTR stabilizers (tafamidis), gene silencers
•
AL amyloidosis: Novel chemotherapy combinations
•
Sarcoidosis: Advanced immunosuppressive protocols

Future Directions

•
Gene-based therapies: For genetic RCM forms
•
Anti-fibrotic agents: Clinical trials ongoing
•
Mechanical support: Devices for RCM patients

Research Focus

  • • Systematic reviews improving diagnostic accuracy
  • • Meta-analyses of imaging biomarkers
  • • Genetic studies expanding gene associations
  • • Standardized diagnostic criteria development

Translational Research

  • • Molecular mechanism understanding
  • • Translational research emphasis
  • • Well-designed clinical trials
  • • Precision medicine approaches

Clinical Impact

  • • Enhanced risk stratification
  • • Better treatment outcomes
