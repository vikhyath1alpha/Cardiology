Executive Summary

Pediatric myocarditis represents a complex inflammatory condition of the heart muscle with highly variable clinical presentation, ranging from subclinical disease to fulminant heart failure and sudden cardiac death. This comprehensive review synthesizes current evidence on etiology, diagnosis, management, and outcomes.

Key Findings

  • Viral infections, particularly Parvovirus B19 and enteroviruses, remain the predominant cause
  • Cardiac MRI with Lake Louise Criteria achieves 87.5% diagnostic sensitivity
  • Novel biomarkers SII >1050 and SIRI >1.9 predict fulminant disease
  • Immunomodulatory therapy shows promise in severe pediatric cases

Clinical Implications

  • High clinical suspicion required due to non-specific presentations
  • Early recognition and supportive care critical for outcomes
  • Advanced imaging reduces need for invasive biopsy
  • Long-term follow-up essential due to risk of DCM progression

1. Etiology and Pathogenesis

1.1 Common Etiological Agents

Myocarditis in children is predominantly caused by viral infections, with a diverse range of other infectious and non-infectious agents implicated [1], [2]. Recent data suggest Parvovirus B19 is the most common virus isolated in children with myocarditis [131].

Etiological Category Specific Agents/Examples Clinical Notes
Viral Enteroviruses, Parvovirus B19, HHV-6, Adenovirus, SARS-CoV-2 Most common cause; PVB19 predominant in recent reports
Bacterial H. influenzae, Streptococcus, Lyme carditis Less common than viral causes
Fungal Histoplasma, Candida Rare, typically in immunocompromised hosts
Protozoal T. cruzi (Chagas), Rickettsial species Regionally endemic diseases
Non-Infectious Autoimmune diseases, Drug reactions, Toxins Diverse mechanisms; giant cell myocarditis has poor prognosis
COVID-19 Related SARS-CoV-2 infection, mRNA vaccines Higher risk from infection vs. vaccine in children

Key Insight

Myocarditis shows bimodal age distribution with peaks in infancy and adolescence, and higher incidence in males (1.5:1 to 1.8:1 ratio) and Black/Hispanic children [5].

1.2 Pathophysiological Mechanisms

Viral Entry and Replication

Specific cellular receptors facilitate viral entry: Coxsackie-adenoviral receptor (CAR) for enteroviruses and erythrocyte P-antigen receptor for Parvovirus B19 [4].

Innate Immune Activation

Infected cells release DAMPs, triggering pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6) and recruiting neutrophils and monocytes [2].

Adaptive Immune Response

Antigen-specific T-lymphocytes (CD4+, CD8+) and B-lymphocytes contribute to viral clearance but also cause myocardial damage through cytokine production and direct cytotoxicity.

Progression to DCM

Chronic inflammation leads to progressive fibrosis, adverse ventricular remodeling, and development of dilated cardiomyopathy [4].

1.3 Sex Differences and Immune Response

Myocarditis exhibits male predominance (1.5:1 to 1.8:1 ratio) across pediatric age groups [5]. Experimental evidence suggests estrogen may have cardioprotective effects by attenuating inflammatory responses, while androgens might exacerbate inflammation [133].

2. Clinical Presentation and Diagnosis

2.1 Spectrum of Clinical Manifestations

Clinical presentation is highly variable, ranging from asymptomatic to fulminant heart failure and sudden cardiac death [1]. Up to 83% of children require multiple healthcare visits before diagnosis [7].

Presentation Type Symptoms & Signs Key Characteristics
Subclinical Incidental ECG/Echo abnormalities Discovered during evaluation for other conditions
Acute Non-Fulminant Fever, fatigue, feeding intolerance, tachycardia Most common presentation; symptoms develop over days-weeks
Fulminant Sudden severe heart failure, cardiogenic shock Rapid onset within 1-2 days; better recovery if survived
Chronic/DCM Persistent heart failure, exercise intolerance May represent progression from undiagnosed acute cases
Arrhythmia-Predominant Palpitations, syncope, sustained VT Can occur without significant ventricular dysfunction
Sudden Cardiac Death Often no preceding symptoms Diagnosis often made post-mortem

2.2 Diagnostic Workup

Cardiac Biomarkers

  • • Troponin I/T (myocardial injury)
  • • BNP/NT-proBNP (heart failure)
  • • Elevated but non-specific

Electrocardiography

  • • Sinus tachycardia
  • • ST-T wave changes
  • • Arrhythmias, low voltage

Echocardiography

  • • LV dysfunction/dilation
  • • Regional wall motion abnormalities
  • • Myocardial strain imaging

2.3 Advanced Imaging: Cardiac MRI

Revised Lake Louise Criteria (2018)

Requires one T1-based criterion AND one T2-based criterion for diagnosis, achieving 87.5% sensitivity with parametric mapping [69].

T1-Based Criteria

  • • Prolonged native T1 mapping
  • • Elevated ECV fraction (≥35% linked to MACE)
  • • Late gadolinium enhancement (non-ischemic pattern)

T2-Based Criteria

  • • Increased T2 relaxation time
  • • High signal intensity on T2-STIR
  • • Elevated myocardium:skeletal muscle ratio

2.4 Endomyocardial Biopsy

EMB remains the historical gold standard but has limited routine use due to invasiveness and sampling error (sensitivity 10-35% with <5 samples) [10].

Current Indications for EMB

  • • Rapidly progressive heart failure unresponsive to therapy
  • • Suspected giant cell or eosinophilic myocarditis
  • • Life-threatening arrhythmias of unknown etiology
  • • Failure to improve with standard care

3. Management and Treatment

3.1 Supportive Care and Hemodynamics

Respiratory Support

  • • Maintain O₂ saturations ≥90%
  • • NIPPV (CPAP) for respiratory distress
  • • High-risk intubation: ketamine preferred
  • • Consider pre-induction inotropes

Hemodynamic Management

  • • Milrinone: First-line inotrope (0.5-1 mcg/kg/min)
  • • Judicious fluid management
  • • Vasopressors for vasoplegic shock
  • • ECMO for refractory cases

3.2 Immunomodulatory Therapies

IVIG Therapy

Common in pediatric practice; recent meta-analysis shows improved LVEF and reduced mortality [96].

Mechanisms: Viral neutralization, cytokine modulation, complement inhibition

Corticosteroids

Indicated for immune-mediated forms (giant cell, eosinophilic myocarditis) and severe fulminant cases.

Considerations: May impair viral clearance if given too early

3.3 Antiviral and Specific Therapies

Specific antiviral therapy limited to identified pathogens: HIV, CMV, HHV-6. No proven agents for common causes like Parvovirus B19 or enteroviruses [92].

3.4 Management of Arrhythmias

Unstable Tachyarrhythmias

Immediate cardioversion/defibrillation

Stable Tachyarrhythmias

Antiarrhythmics (amiodarone, lidocaine)

Bradyarrhythmias

Temporary pacing if hemodynamically significant

3.5 Mechanical Circulatory Support

ECMO Considerations

11-55% of pediatric myocarditis patients may require MCS. Early initiation crucial before irreversible end-organ damage [31].

Indications
  • • Refractory cardiogenic shock
  • • Life-threatening arrhythmias
  • • Cardiac arrest
Goals
  • • Bridge to recovery
  • • Bridge to transplantation
  • • Stabilization during arrhythmias

4.3 Risk of Sudden Cardiac Death

SCD Risk Factors

Acute Phase
  • • Malignant ventricular arrhythmias
  • • Extensive myocardial inflammation
  • • Severe conduction system involvement
Chronic Phase
  • • Patchy myocardial fibrosis
  • • Residual scarring
  • • Re-entrant ventricular arrhythmias
Athlete Considerations

Temporary restriction from competitive sports during active myocarditis and recovery period. Return-to-play requires normalization of function, absence of arrhythmias, and resolution of inflammation.

4.4 Recovery of Ventricular Function

Recovery is variable and depends on initial injury severity. Many children experience complete spontaneous recovery, while others progress to chronic DCM [125].

Recovery Patterns

Fulminant myocarditis often shows better complete recovery if acute phase is survived, compared to acute non-fulminant cases that progress to chronic DCM [59].

5.2 Clinical Presentation Variations

Pediatric Presentation

  • • Non-specific symptoms predominant
  • • Fever, fatigue, poor feeding
  • • Respiratory distress (misdiagnosed as pneumonia)
  • • GI symptoms, hepatomegaly
  • • Fulminant presentation more common
  • • Systemic inflammatory syndrome features

Adult Presentation

  • • ACS-like symptoms common
  • • Acute chest pain predominant
  • • Ischemic ECG changes
  • • Elevated cardiac biomarkers
  • • More localized cardiac symptoms
  • • Fewer extra-cardiac manifestations

5.3 Management Approach Differences

Key Management Differences

Pediatric Approach
  • • More aggressive hemodynamic support
  • • Higher use of immunomodulatory therapies
  • • More frequent antiviral therapy
  • • Lower threshold for ECMO
  • • Age-specific dosing considerations
Adult Approach
  • • More conventional cardiac care initially
  • • Immunomodulation for specific subtypes
  • • Focus on coronary evaluation
  • • Different MCS considerations
  • • Comorbidity management

5.4 Prognostic Outcomes

A 2025 study found no significant age-related disparity in 30-day major adverse cardiac events (18.1% children vs 13.4% adults) [138].

Unique Pediatric Considerations

  • • Higher rates of heart failure and cardiogenic shock at presentation
  • • Extra-cardiac symptoms associated with increased MACE risk
  • • Fulminant myocarditis more common but better recovery potential

6. Recent Research and Guidelines (2022-Present)

6.1 Key Findings from Recent Studies

Novel Inflammatory Indices

SII >1050 and SIRI >1.9 within first 6 hours predict fulminant myocarditis and adverse outcomes [35].

Derived from routine CBC parameters (neutrophils, lymphocytes, platelets)

Biomarker Analysis

DNI, ESR, CRP, AST, CK-MB, NT-proBNP, and troponin-T significantly different between spontaneous recovery and ECMO-requiring groups [43].

Nationwide Study Findings

Large prospective study (328 children vs 417 adults) showed children exhibit more clinical and biological inflammatory syndromes, extra-cardiac manifestations, and severe presentations requiring increased support [138].

6.4 COVID-19 and Vaccines Impact

COVID-19 Myocarditis

Children at greater risk from COVID-19 infection than from mRNA vaccines. Myocarditis can occur as acute illness or part of MIS-C.

SARS-CoV-2 Infection
  • • Higher risk in children
  • • Can be severe in MIS-C
  • • Requires immunomodulatory treatment
mRNA Vaccines
  • • Typically mild cases
  • • Rapid clinical recovery
  • • Benefits outweigh risks

6.5 Recent Expert Consensus and Guidelines

2021 AHA Scientific Statement

Strong endorsement of Cardiac MRI with revised Lake Louise Criteria for diagnosing and confirming acute myocarditis in children [89].

Emphasis on ventricular function assessment and tissue characterization (edema, hyperemia, necrosis, fibrosis)

2022 SCMR Expert Consensus

Guidelines for CMR in acquired pediatric heart disease, including parametric mapping techniques and myocardial strain imaging [61].
